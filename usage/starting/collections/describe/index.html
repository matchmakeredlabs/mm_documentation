How to Describe Elements?

Now that you have established an excellent hierarchy for your collection, you are ready to begin describing its elements; that is, painting a description for each one using MatchMaker’s PaletTM statements!

In this step, you will learn how to generate a PaletTM Key representing the intent of the element you are describing. This Palet Key is part of an element’s “Descriptor”, a fancy name for all the information attached to an element. This is where the magic of MatchMaker happens and enables all the powerful insights to be explored in the next section: “How to Generate a Report?”.

Math Help: Describing the Math Help Collection​

In an incredible escalation, Malia herself shows up to Math Help headquarters, begging for assistance with quadratic equations.. Mrs. MathHelp storms into your office (again), and demands that you get the quadratic equation’s section done YESTERDAY. Fortunately, you're not sweating it as MatchMaker makes this critical process easy. You start at the MatchMaker dashboard.

Press “Browse & Describe” to begin.

Now, find your collection. Click on the field that says “Enter Keyword…” and type in “Math Help”, then press ENTER or “+ Filter” to search for the Math Help collection.

3.

Click on “Math Help’s Mathematical Resources” to view its elements and start describing them!

Currently, your hierarchy is collapsed, in this collection you can press “Expand” twice to fully display the elements of the collection.

tip

Don’t forget to use the shapes to guide you through the collection format. Elements with squares have no elements attached to them and can be described. Meanwhile, triangles represent elements which can be expanded to reveal others (and cannot be described).

Describing our First Element​

Begin by selecting the “Completing the Square” element; to do this, click on its name.

Now that the element is selected, press “Edit Description” to enter the describe view. There’s a lot going on this page, let’s break it down.

info

1.
This is your portal to the PaletTM, here you can search for the Palet statements that you’ll need to build a Palet key for your element, in essence, the little concepts you’ll need to combine to represent a full piece of information

      a. You can add context to your search

            ii. + Descriptor: The description of your element will be used as context in tandem with your search query

            iii. + Context: The hierarchy will be used as context in tandem with your search query

      b. You can switch the mode of your search

            i. Eco: Use a standard, quick search algorithm

            ii. AI: Use an advanced AI-enabled search for more intelligent results

      c. Every Palet statement has an assigned unique identifying number (ID), a type, and a little piece of knowledge (its statement)

            i. The types are as follows:

                  1. Skills, Tools, Fact knowledge (STFk): concepts that underlie a discipline

                  2. Conceptual Understanding (CU): High level concepts that often incorporate many different concepts and are organizationally essential to a discipline

                  3. Process (P): a method of accomplishing a certain task

2.
Here is your element’s descriptor, which is all the information about your element. Here all of the Palet statements you select will go to build your Palet key

Begin by scrolling down on the descriptor view on the right until you see a label at the bottom that says “Key”. This is your Palet key, and it will grow as you give it Palet statements. So, let's give it a first Palet statement! To do this, search for “Completing the Square” in Text mode and click on the magnifying glass.

Once the magnifying glass is clicked, MatchMaker will search through the Palet for a matching Palet statement to “Completing the Square”, yielding the following result:

This statement greatly helps describe your current lesson, hence, press the “+” icon to add it to our element’s Palet key.

Great, you have successfully added your first Palet statement! It now appears under the Palet key for “Completing the Square”, in essence, you’ve stated that this little piece of knowledge is addressed by this lesson. For simplicity’s sake, leave the element with just this Palet statement, and more will be added later. For now, scroll back up on the Descriptor side until you see a “Save” button.

Click on it to save our changes, and congratulations! You’ve just described your first element, and begun painting a wonderful canvas of Math Help knowledge.

After saving our changes, the hierarchy has updated the icons next to elements to show that we have described “Completing the Square”(this can be seen after refreshing the page where we previewed our whole collection).

Central vs Peripheral Palet Statements​

You have a solid PaletTM key for “Quadratic Form”, but some of these statements don’t quite fit Math Helps resources. As you know, Mrs. Math Help loves to touch on some broader topics in Math Help resources, while keeping things pretty simple. However, it would be wrong to ignore Mrs. Math Help’s addition of some broader learning on this topic. Don’t worry, MatchMaker has a system to handle this. Every Palet statement can be marked as either central or peripheral. For example, a lesson on how to cook scrambled eggs covers the process of cracking eggs, very directly, that is, it is a central topic. However, the addition of oil or how to use a spatula isn’t likely directly taught or even the focus of the lesson; but it is covered to a degree. Therefore, that is a peripheral concept in that lesson.

In “Quadratic Form”, “identifying functions as linear, exponential, quadratic, absolute value, logarithmic, or rational” is never explicitly discussed, but is related to the topics at hand, and covered to a degree. Therefore, let’s mark this statement as peripheral. We do this by unchecking the circled box above under the “Cen” or central label, therefore marking it as peripheral.

Now, scroll up and press save!

Describing a Complex Element​

Go ahead and press the right arrow (→) to move onto the last element (“Graphing Quadratic Equations”).

As you may remember from school, this is actually a pretty big topic. Graphing Quadratic Equations touches on many different bits of knowledge; you need to know how to graph a function, convert the equation to an easy form, and more. Therefore, this is a fairly complex element to describe and you may also have elements like this in the future. To describe this element, it is best to use the upcoming process ahead: picking out any matching Palet statements, weeding out less useful ones, and marking statements as central or peripheral. Let's begin by performing a search for the topic and its related items; and you’ll find lots and lots of matches. You will now add anything that even remotely fits, and then, once you are done gathering PaletTM statements, you will remove whatever fits worst. To go back to the metaphor of color, you will go to the artist's palette, get many colors, then see how they mix together and return the ones that don’t quite fit. If you remember some math, this is a very useful example to try on our own. Some good search queries to use are: “parabola”, and the name of an element is never a bad bet, “Graphing Quadratic Equations”.

Describing an Element using AI and Context​

Now that you've gone through and described all the elements using MatchMaker's most simple search, let's make things a little bit easier, and finish describing the first element, "Completing the Square", using MatchMaker's AI search and element context.

Press the left arrow (←) three times to return to "Completing the Square" (or until you receive an error).

Now, press "+ Descriptor" and "+ Context" for the search to take into account the element's description and the hierarchy around it. Then, click on the toggle switch between "Eco" and "AI" to switch to AI search.

After some loading, you will receive the above results. Feel free to scroll through the results and add what fits a "Completing the Square" section the most.

tip

Remember the three step technique used in "Describing a Complex Element":

  1. Sift through the PaletTM for any matching Palet statements
  2. Cut down on Palet statements; remove ones that fit poorly, while maintaining those that fit better
  3. Mark the remaining Palet statements as central or peripheral
